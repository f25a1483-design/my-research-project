The Bioscience Challenge

In Malaysia, food security relies heavily on self-sufficiency in rice production. The elite variety MR297 (released by the Malaysian Agricultural Research and Development Institute - MARDI) has a short maturity period of 105 to 111 days and a high yield potential of up to 9.5 tonnes per hectare.

To optimize crop yield and prevent luxury consumption or wasting of Nitrogen fertilizer, agronomic researchers use mathematical modeling. The cumulative dry shoot biomass ($g/m^2$) of the plant follows a S-shaped Logistic Growth Model.

"By calculating the first-order derivative of this curve, we identify the exact day of maximum vegetative activity (inflection point). This is the key biological marker indicating when the plant's nutritional demand is highest."

Mathematical Formulation

1. Cumulative Biomass Model $f(t)$
$$f(t) = \frac{L}{1 + e^{-k(t - t_0)}}$$
2. Absolute Growth Rate (Derivative) $f'(t)$
$$f'(t) = \frac{L \cdot k \cdot e^{-k(t - t_0)}}{(1 + e^{-k(t - t_0)})^2}$$
Derived also as $f'(t) = k \cdot f(t) \cdot \left(1 - \frac{f(t)}{L}\right)$ (in $g/m^2/\text{day}$)
$t$: Days After Sowing (DAS)
$L$: Max Biomass Cap ($g/m^2$)
$k$: Growth Coeff. ($\text{day}^{-1}$)
$t_0$: Max Growth Day (Inflection)
